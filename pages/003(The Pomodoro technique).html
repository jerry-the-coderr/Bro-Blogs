Beat Procrastination with the Pomodoro Technique

Saturday, 07 July 2025

We’ve all been there. You want to study, exercise, start a project — but somehow, you just can’t get yourself to start. It’s like there’s an invisible wall between you and the first step. If this sounds familiar, then the Pomodoro Technique might be exactly what you need.

This technique is super simple, yet surprisingly effective. It helps you break down your work into small, focused sessions with regular breaks in between. This keeps your brain fresh, reduces burnout, and makes starting things a lot less scary.

What is the Pomodoro Technique?
The Pomodoro Technique is a time management method created by an Italian student named Francesco Cirillo in the 1980s. He used a kitchen timer shaped like a tomato (called a “pomodoro” in Italian) to time his study sessions. The core idea is to work in short, focused bursts followed by short breaks. These work sessions are called “pomodoros.”

Typically, you work for 25 minutes straight, then take a 5-minute break. After completing four pomodoros, you get a longer break of about 15 to 30 minutes. It’s simple, structured, and keeps your brain from getting fried.

How to Use It:

  • Pick a task you want to work on. (It can be studying, writing, cleaning your room, anything.)
  • Set a timer for 25 minutes.
  • Work on the task with zero distractions. (Yes — no phone, no Insta scroll.)
  • When the timer rings, stop and take a 5-minute break.
  • Repeat the cycle. After 4 pomodoros, take a longer 15–30 minute break.

Why Does It Work?
Our brains aren’t made for marathon work sessions. Most people lose focus after about 20 minutes. That’s where the Pomodoro Technique helps — it uses short, focused bursts that feel achievable. Knowing you only need to focus for 25 minutes makes it easier to start.

Plus, those short breaks give your mind time to rest and reset. Instead of forcing yourself to sit for 3 hours and end up scrolling reels halfway through, you work in intervals and actually get stuff done.

The Pomodoro Technique also improves time awareness. You start noticing how long tasks actually take, which helps you plan better in the future. And with every completed pomodoro, you get a small sense of achievement — which feels pretty good, too.

Final Thoughts:
So if you’ve been struggling to start or stick to your study sessions, workouts, or side projects, give the Pomodoro Technique a shot. All you need is a timer (your phone will do) and the will to start. Work for 25 minutes, take a break, and repeat. You might be surprised how much you can get done, one pomodoro at a time.
